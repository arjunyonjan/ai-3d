🎮 GAME DEV NOTES

Three.js techniques used in the JARVIS Memory Pyramid

1. HITBOX

Layman meaning: An invisible box or ball attached to a thing. When you click, the game checks if your click touched this invisible shape — not the visible art.
① A character in a fighting game has a visible body, but the hitbox is an invisible rectangle around the torso. Punches check the hitbox, not the pixels.
② A button on a website looks like a rounded rectangle, but the browser's hitbox is the rectangular clickable area. Click outside it = nothing happens.
③ In a car racing game, each car has a hidden hitbox box around it. When two boxes overlap = crash. The visual car model can have spikes or wings that don't affect collision.
④ A light switch on the wall has a small plastic toggle, but the hitbox is a larger rectangle behind it. You don't need to perfectly hit the tiny switch — the game helps you.
⑤ In the JARVIS pyramid, folder labels are HTML text, but the hitbox is a hidden 3D box behind them. The click feels natural because the invisible box matches the text size exactly.

CSS2D labels are HTML overlays — Three.js Raycaster cannot detect them. Solution: spawn invisible 3D meshes ("ghosts") at the same position.

  • FOLDER BoxGeometry(w, h, 0.3) — size computed from actual label pixels ÷ camera projection
  • FEATURE BoxGeometry(2.5, 1.5, 0.3) — fixed size for feature rows
  • Material: MeshBasicMaterial({ opacity: 0, depthTest: false }) — fully invisible, no z-fighting

2. RAYCASTER

Layman meaning: A virtual laser beam shot from the camera (your eyes) through the mouse position into the 3D world. Whatever it hits first gets clicked.
① Point a laser pointer at a wall of toys. The first toy the laser dot lands on is the one you "clicked." That's the raycaster.
② In an FPS game, when you aim down sights and shoot, the game fires a ray from your gun barrel forward. The first enemy the ray hits takes damage — even if they're far away.
③ In a strategy game, clicking on the ground sends a ray from camera through the mouse. Where it hits the ground plane = the move-to position for your unit.
④ A barcode scanner at a supermarket shoots a red laser (ray). It reads the first barcode it hits. Same idea — shoot, detect, return the first match.
⑤ In JARVIS pyramid, clicking dispatches a ray from the camera through your mouse position. It tests every invisible hitbox and returns the nearest one. That's how we know you clicked "AI-3" and not something behind it.

Converts mouse screen position → 3D ray → tests intersection with ghost meshes.

renderer.domElement.addEventListener('click', (e) => {
  pointer.x = (e.clientX / w) * 2 - 1;
  pointer.y = -(e.clientY / h) * 2 + 1;
  raycaster.setFromCamera(pointer, camera);
  const hits = raycaster.intersectObjects(cls);
  if (hits.length) toggleProject(hits[0].object);
});

3. LERP

Layman meaning: Short for "linear interpolation." It means smoothly moving from point A to point B over time — like sliding a volume knob instead of snapping it.
① A sliding door: instead of vanishing and reappearing at the other end, it slides smoothly from open to closed. That slide is a lerp.
② An elevator going from floor 3 to floor 7: it doesn't teleport. It lerps upward over 3 seconds, passing every floor in between.
③ Fading a light from 0% to 100% brightness: the dimmer switch lerps the voltage — smooth, not a sudden on/off.
④ In a cutscene, the camera lerps from a wide shot to a close-up over 2 seconds. Your eyes follow naturally instead of jumping.
⑤ In JARVIS pyramid, when you click a project, the camera lerps from its current position to a close-up over 1 second using a cubic ease curve. It doesn't teleport — it flies there smoothly.

Smooth camera transitions using lerpVectors with cubic ease-out: 1 - (1 - t)³.

const ease = 1 - Math.pow(1 - p, 3);
camera.position.lerpVectors(_from, _to, ease);
orbitControls.target.lerpVectors(_targetFrom, _targetTo, ease);

4. SPRITE

Layman meaning: A flat image that always faces the camera. No matter how you rotate the 3D world, the sprite turns to stare at you — like a cardboard cutout on a rotating platform.
① A cardboard cutout of a celebrity at a movie theater — it's flat, but from the front it looks 3D. If you walk left, it still faces you (it's on a pivot). That's a sprite.
② In old 2D games (Mario, Sonic), every character was a sprite — a flat image that always faced the screen. The game world was 2D, but the sprites felt alive.
③ Health bars floating above character heads in games: they're sprites that always face your camera so you can read them from any angle.
④ A glowing light bulb in a 3D scene: instead of modeling the glass and filament, use a bright circular sprite that always faces you. Same visual, zero geometry cost.
⑤ In JARVIS pyramid, the glowing green halos around the top 5 recent projects are sprites — 128×128 radial gradient images that always face the camera. They pulse, glow, and cost almost no GPU power.

Camera-facing billboards using CanvasTexture with radial gradients.

  • HALO 128×128 radial gradient, AdditiveBlending, pulsing opacity 0.6–1.0
  • FEATURE 64×64 radial gradient, red for fail / cyan for success, pulsing + scale oscillation

5. ADDITIVE BLENDING

Layman meaning: A glow mode where colors add together. Two overlapping glows become brighter where they meet — like shining two flashlights on the same spot turns it whiter.
① Two flashlights shining on a wall: where both beams overlap, the spot is brighter than either alone. That's additive blending — light adds to light.
② A neon sign in a dark alley: the glow spills outward. Where two neon tubes cross, the glow is whiter because the red and blue light add together.
③ Photoshop layers set to "Screen" mode: white areas let everything through, black blocks nothing. Overlapping semi-transparent areas get brighter. That's additive.
④ In a dark video game, a lava pool uses additive blending. Where two lava sprites overlap, the orange glow becomes yellow-white — hotter, brighter, more intense.
⑤ In JARVIS pyramid, the green glow halos use AdditiveBlending. Where two project nodes are close, their halos merge into a bright cyan hotspot — creating the illusion of connected energy.

6. GHOST

Layman meaning: An invisible 3D shape that exists purely to detect clicks. You never see it — it's the "ghost" of a visible object, doing the hidden work.
① Invisible walls in video games: you see a beautiful canyon, but there's a ghost wall blocking you from walking through a gap. You can't see it, but your character bumps into it.
② A motion sensor light: the ghost is the invisible cone of detection. When you walk into it — click, light on. The cone isn't visible, only its effect is.
③ In platformer games, a coin has a ghost sphere around it. You don't need to touch the pixel-perfect coin art — just get close enough, and the ghost sphere catches your collision.
④ A Wi-Fi router broadcasts an invisible signal sphere. Your phone connects when it's inside that sphere. The router's plastic shell is visible, but the connection zone is a ghost.
⑤ In JARVIS pyramid, each folder name text has a ghost BoxGeometry behind it. The text is HTML (invisible to 3D raycaster), but the ghost box catches the ray and registers the click.

7. DRAG vs CLICK DISAMBIGUATION

Layman meaning: A trick to tell whether the user meant to drag (move something) or click (select something). If your finger moved too far before lifting, it was a drag, not a click.
① On a phone, tapping an app icon opens it. But if you drag it slightly, it enters "jiggle mode." The phone checks: did your finger move more than 10px before lifting? If yes, it's a drag, not a tap.
② In a file browser, clicking selects the file. Dragging it moves it. The OS tracks your mouse: if you moved more than 4 pixels while the button was down, it switches to drag mode.
③ A light switch: pressing it down = click. Wiping your hand across multiple switches = drag. The mechanism detects whether your finger stayed in one spot or traveled.
④ In chess, clicking a piece selects it (highlight). But if you click-and-drag the piece to a square, it moves there. The game waits: did you release without moving (click) or drag to a new square (move)?
⑤ In JARVIS pyramid, when you pointer-down on a node, it records the XY position. On click (release), if the mouse moved more than 10px, it was a drag — ignore the click. This prevents accidental toggles while dragging nodes around.

8. FOLLOWER PATTERN

Layman meaning: A list of "update my position" instructions stored on each object. When the object moves, it runs through the list and tells everything attached to it to move too.
① A mother duck with ducklings: the mother moves, and each duckling has a built-in "follow mother" instruction. No one tells ducklings individually — they just execute their follower behavior.
② A train: the engine moves, and each carriage has a coupling that says "follow the thing in front of you." The engine doesn't push each carriage — the couplings (followers) handle it.
③ A PowerPoint group: if you group 5 shapes and drag the group, all 5 move together. Each shape is a follower of the group's position.
④ A person walking a dog: the leash is the follower connection. When the person moves, the dog follows. Different dogs (objects) use the same leash pattern.
⑤ In JARVIS pyramid, each draggable node stores a list of _followers: its label, its glow halo, its feature lines, and its web lines. Drag the node once, and all followers update automatically. No if-else chains per object type.

9. ISOLATION MODE

Layman meaning: "Focus mode." Click an object → everything else disappears, camera zooms in on it. Press BACK → everything returns. Like selecting one file out of 100 and putting the rest in a drawer.
① A museum spotlight: in a dark room, one painting is lit. The others are in shadow. You can only see the selected exhibit. Step back = lights come back on. That's isolation.
② A police detective's evidence board: they pull one photo off and pin it to a solo board, removing all distractions. Focus on one clue at a time. BACK returns to the full wall.
③ In a game, selecting a unit in an RTS: all other units dim or the camera follows only that unit. Deselect = all units visible again.
④ Full-screen mode on a video: the video takes over your entire screen. Everything else hides. Press ESC = your desktop returns. One focus, no distractions.
⑤ In JARVIS pyramid, click a project node → all other 42 nodes fade out, the selected node's glow shrinks, camera flies close. A BACK button appears. Click it → all nodes restore, camera returns to the overview.

10. Z-DEPTH PUSH (SEARCH FADE)

Layman meaning: When searching, matching items stay close to you; non-matching items get pushed far back and become almost invisible. Like a spotlight on the search results.
① A librarian searching for a book: she pulls matching books to the front of the shelf and pushes non-matching ones to the back. You only see the ones that match your query.
② A stage with 50 actors: the director calls "only actors wearing red, step forward." The red-clad actors step toward the spotlight; others step into darkness. Audience sees only red.
③ Ctrl+F in a browser: matching words get highlighted (yellow). Non-matching text stays normal. The highlight = pull forward. Everything else stays put.
④ A dating app filter: set "age 25-30" → profiles outside that range get pushed to the bottom of the stack. You only swipe through the filtered ones.
⑤ In JARVIS pyramid, type a search query. Matching projects stay at their original Z position at full opacity. Non-matching ones get pushed 50 units backward and fade to 3% opacity. They're still there but effectively invisible.

GLOSSARY

  • Hitbox — invisible collision volume for click/drag detection (fighting game term, 1990s)
  • Raycaster — laser beam from camera through mouse that finds the clicked object
  • Lerp — smooth slide from A to B over time (not a teleport)
  • Sprite — flat image that always faces the camera (cardboard cutout)
  • AdditiveBlending — glow mode where overlaps get brighter (flashlights on a wall)
  • Ghost — invisible 3D shape that exists only to catch clicks
  • CSS2DObject — HTML label pinned to a 3D position in the scene
  • Follower — a callback stored on each object that updates attached elements on drag
  • Isolation — select one, hide all others, zoom camera in
  • Z-Depth Push — push non-matching items far away during search (fade + depth)
